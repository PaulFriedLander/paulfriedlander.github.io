Many Worlds

Many Worlds is an idea introduced by Hugh Everett as an interpretation of quantum mechanics. He proposed it as a way to make quantum mechanics deterministic. He showed that when the equations show that several different events are possible, it could be argued that all the possibilities happen, each in their own separate universe. This interpretation would preserve the unitary development of the wave function in a smooth and continuous way so that quantum mechanics does not imply random processes are fundamental. What remains as random is which particular branch of the universe we happen to find ourselves in.

The branching appears to lead to an exponential number of new universes continuously being created, including multiple copies of myself all writing slightly different versions of this article. Max Tegmark dismisses this criticism. He argues that the number of universes is finite and that the parallel worlds are mostly very similar and frequently merge as well. He considers the number of universes not a problem at all. Viewed from a timeless point of view, as Barbour would, all possible configurations of the universe exist eternally so there is no growth at all. David Deutsch, who pionered much of the early theoretical research in quantum computing, is also a strong supporter of Many Worlds. He argues that the development of quantum computers is evidence in its favour as the power of the qubit depends upon its ability to interact with itself in those other universes.

I would suggest that Many Worlds is necessary for time travel and that you cannot have one without the other. The alternative is that if the Many Worlds do not exist and time travel is possible, the Universe must be perfectly deterministic and we do not have free will. The existence of free will most people take as fundamental, although no surprise to find that some physicists would disagree.

I conjecture that Many Worlds will act as a filter blocking certain types of information from being sent back from the future. We would receive messages from numerous different versions of the future and any variations that are random, perhaps for example the future state of the stock market as described in Implications, would become blurred by the overlapping of the different messages. Our instruments would produce ambiguous readings when we tried to receive any purely stochastic information. In contrast a process that is presently beyond human understanding but for which there is almost certainly is a perfectly good deterministic theory, say being able to predict earthquakes, the warning should come through very clearly as in any version of the World that is substantially similar to this one, earthquakes will still happen at the same moments.

Let us now consider another kind of information arriving from the future: science and technology. Unlike the stock prices this will remain accurate even after the information is freely and openly available. We can imagine that in this situation, many workers will seek to reproduce methods and results already known about in the future. This introduces us to the yet another variant on the familiar paradoxes of time travel. Is this the show stopper or can we still push ahead with this line of reasoning? Who, it will be asked, originated an idea if the first to work on it learnt about the concept in papers not yet published. Will they find their own names on those papers or other? It looks like cheating. It must be admitted that there are far more unanswered questions at this point than clear answers.

I am predicting that the presence of many worlds will be a key factor in determining what information we can receive and what gets scrambled in its journey from the future. Whether this involves cheating or not in the way I have just described, I am not sure. As an artist I have always had the intuition that I am already in contact with the future. My suspicion is that receiving information from the future is one of the senses of the unconscious mind. Most people do not notice if it happens or do not have the gift, but precognition, in one form or another is widely recognised outside the world of hard science. For many ideas come in dreams but it can happen just easily when we are wide awake. I would not pretend to be able to will this sense or direct it in any way yet I feel convinced that it has been a channel for inspiration, and on at least one occasion for more direct information too.
