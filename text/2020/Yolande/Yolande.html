Art by Yolande Friedlander (1914 - 1967)

My mother was an artist, I recently rediscovered most of these paintings while going through posessions stored by my sister. Sadly even while she lived much of this work, far from being appreciated, got consigned to the garage and only now have I brought it back to the light of day. Some are damaged, some I remember and strike strange emotions in me as I have not seen them since I was a small child and she would have been painting them.

The image above is one of the ones that I remembered. I thought it was about 4 feet high (1.2 metres).

Actual size 57 x 36 cm acrylic on hardboard. The white speckles on the left are mould damage.

This dark image with a black form within a figure I suspect my mother painted at some point after she got her cancer diagnosis, which was in her mid 40s. At some point later in her illness, she largely gave up painting but continued with ceramics and print making. (To be documented later) This painting is particularly badly damaged by mould, white and orange speckles.

The portraits of my father were painted much earlier, probably while we were still living in Manchester (1954) or even before I was born. (1951)

The portraits above are of Marie, my mother's best friend. She lived a few doors away from our home in Cambridge. I looked up the name in Google, it says: Many believe it to mean "sea of bitterness" or "sea of sorrow." However some sources cite the alternative definitions of "rebellion"...and "mistress or lady of the sea." She was indeed the wildest character on the street, growing tired of her bank manager husband, she allegedly took first a male lover and then a woman, sub dividing the house and settling down with her new lesbian partner, while her husband was confined to his quarters upstairs.

These two harbour landscapes were likely painted in Concarneau in Brittany around 1950, shortly before I was born, the last chance my parents had to feel like young lovers and finally free also from the weight of living through the war and the worst of the post was shortages. This must have been a happy and tranquil time.

Some of these pictures are painted one on either side of a piece of board. A sure sign she was experimenting and not necassarily so fond of the works.

This picture above is another I remember well from the time she painted it, again I expected it to be much larger than its actual dimensions. A wonderful way memories from early childhood play tricks making things appear larger in recollection than in reality.
