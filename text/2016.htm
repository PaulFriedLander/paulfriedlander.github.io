Flame Stadium:

The Olympic Flame on an Olympian scale.

Key to this idea is pushing materials beyond limits they have ever been used before. This would be the largest carbon fibre structure ever constructed. Until now carbon fibre has chiefly been used in special components, for example the blades of giant windmills and parts of aeroplanes. Increasingly airplane manufacturers have come to consider that the next generation of planes will be entirely carbon fibre. Will this change happen in architecture too? The answer in most cases is no, the extra cost would not justify it. One exception I propose will be the construction of giant towers where the astonishing strength to weight ratio of carbon fibre as compared with steel will actually make it cheaper to work in carbon even though kilo for kilo it is still far more expensive. How is this possible?

I came to realise that this would be the case by comparison with the engineering of a space rocket. For every kilo of payload you take to orbit, you need something of the order of thirty kilos of fuel and oxidant. Although some of that is burnt early on, a significant fraction has to be carried with you most of the way into orbit. If you can increase the efficiency of the rocket, you reduce the amount of fuel and oxidiser you need to carry and this can have a dramatic impact on the amount of payload. A small improvement in rocket design can greatly increase the payload.

When we compare the situation with constructing a tower, a similar logic applies. Imagine a structure say initially 10 metres high built of steel. If we want to add another ten metres on top, the first ten metres must be strong enough to take the extra weight of the next ten. Go onto consider a further ten metres bringing the height to thirty metres and the lower sections have to be stronger still. The higher you go, the stronger the lower sections have to be to support the weight above. The total weight increases exponentially with height, but if you can choose a lighter material with comparable strength, the total weight can be far less. The strength to weight ratio of carbon fibre is about five times better than steel, but because of this accumulative advantage, the total difference in weight between a tower built of carbon and steel will be far greater. I have estimated that if the Flame was built of steel to a height of 150 metres, it would need more than a thousand tons of steel. With carbon the weight might be only forty tons or less. The exact weight is hard to estimate as it is likely that wind forces will predominate and a final decision would revolve around judging how strong a storm the Flame would need to withstand.

I plan the Flame shall be an open structure constructed of an interlacing framework of carbon fibre tubes. This will keep to the absolute minimum its wind resistance. The structure is not intended to be strong enough to support the weight of people (except service personnel who would only ascend in fine weather.) The only 'payload' it would have to support would be the lighting system.

The lighting will be LEDs. These are rapidly becoming not the lights of the future but the new lights of today. We have seen astonishing technical progress in their brightness and efficiency in the last two years and in the coming years this will be followed by their widespread adoption and drop in price. The Flame can be lit by literally millions of LEDs and this can be provided at a very reasonable cost and would not consume very much energy as LEDs are rapidly becoming the most efficient light source in existence.

For the control of the lighting I imagine the individual LEDs working like pixels on a screen. The technology for this already exists and is being used on a smaller scale on existing LED installation but because it is based upon protocols already developed that are compatible with very high resolution screens, the control systems can readily be upgraded to achieve control over the Flame's vast array.

The lights shall be omni-directional. They would be seen brilliantly from a distance in all directions. Close up they would shine even brighter. The lights would be bright enough to be seen by day, but they would really come into their own by night, when they would literally burn as brightly as a real flame. The Flame would be visible from very many kilometres away. Flying to Madrid by night, the Flame could be seen from over a hundred kilometres away.

I imagine the Flame built on columns. The columns would be laid out in a circular plan; each column about ten metes high. The interlacing framework of carbon tubes would be mounted on the columns. There would be a concourse under the Flame which the public will be free to walk into with the Flame over their heads. This circular space will be about forty metres in diameter: an area large enough to use as an open air concert hall with room to seat a symphony orchestra and fair sized audience. The orchestra would be for special occasions; in general I would like to see the space open and free for people to walk in.
